Singapore has some of the fastest mobile networks in Asia — 4G and 5G coverage is excellent, even underground in MRT tunnels. You'll need your passport to register a SIM card.

📱 SIM Card vs eSIM — What's the Difference?

A physical SIM card is the small chip you insert into your phone — you collect it at the airport or a store and slot it in. An eSIM is a digital SIM built into your phone that can be activated remotely — no physical card needed. Check if your phone supports eSIM (most phones from 2021 onwards do) before deciding which to get.

⚠️ Phone Locked? Some phones are network-locked by your home provider, meaning they only work with that provider's SIM. If your phone is locked, a Singapore SIM won't work. Check with your provider before you travel. If it is locked and you can't unlock it, consider renting a portable Wi-Fi hotspot →

📡 Top Providers

Singtel and StarHub are Singapore's top-tier providers with the widest network coverage across the island, including MRT tunnels and more remote areas. Both are highly reliable and well-regarded for tourist SIM plans.

💡 Our Recommendation Pre-ordering your SIM or eSIM before you travel is the way to go. Both Singtel and StarHub offer a $12 tourist SIM or eSIM that includes 100GB of data — more than enough for a week's trip. (To put that in context: even as a heavy phone user, I rarely get through 20GB in an entire month.)

With 100GB in your pocket, you'll never need to hunt for free Wi-Fi — just use your data like you would at home. It's genuinely liberating.

And here's something most people don't think about: when you use a local SIM instead of keeping your home provider's roaming plan active, your phone stops constantly scanning for foreign networks. That one change alone means your battery will last a normal full day — rather than dying by lunch from all that background signal searching. It's a small thing that makes a big difference.

📦 Collecting or Installing

If you order a physical SIM, you can collect it at the airport immediately on arrival — many counters are open 24/7. If you've purchased an eSIM, you'll receive a QR code by email to scan and activate. Either way, only activate your Singapore SIM once you've arrived in Singapore for the best experience.

🛬 At the Airport

Prepare to be wow-ed! Changi Airport isn't just a transit point — it's a full destination. Multiple times voted the world's best airport, and you'll understand why the moment you step off the plane. 😊

🗺️ Terminal Layout

Terminals 1, 2 & 3 are connected to each other and to Jewel by covered walkways and a free Skytrain. T1 is directly attached to Jewel; T2 and T3 are a 7–10 min walk or a free Skytrain ride. You can wheel your trolley onto the Skytrain too!

Terminal 4 is separate — a 10-min free shuttle bus ride from T2 (or about 25 mins walking). Check your ticket to know which terminal you're at.

🗺️ Terminal Map: View the full Changi Airport terminal layout here: Changi Airport Map →

💎 Jewel Changi Airport

Jewel is a spectacular circular shopping mall with 5 above-ground and 2 basement levels. At its heart is the HSBC Rain Vortex — the world's tallest indoor waterfall at 40 metres. It runs daily from 10am to 10pm.

  • Use the digital wayfinder boards — the circular layout is confusing and some levels don't do a full loop. If you get lost, don't worry - same as us.
  • Jewel is open to everyone, no boarding pass needed. Its a public shopping mall not linked to airside transit. So if you want to visit while you've got a few hours spare, you will need to exit immigration and come out - be sure to keep your new boarding pass with you.
  • Free light show at the Rain Vortex nightly at 8pm & 9pm
  • Some restaurants line the inner circle facing the waterfall — which creates a nice ambience but note conversation can be tough with the noise.
📸 Photo Tip Multiple levels give you different perspectives for photos! And for something special, catch the free Skytrain from T2 to T3 and look to your left - thank us later :)

✈️ Airside Attractions (for Transit Passengers)

If you have a layover, don't just sit at the gate. Changi's transit areas have genuinely amazing free attractions:

  • 🦋 Butterfly Garden (T3) — 1,000+ butterflies across ~40 species flying freely. It's humid — intentionally so!
  • 🌻 Sunflower Garden (T2 Rooftop) — A rare outdoor airside space. Fresh air, runway views, and real Singapore sunflowers. Can be hot at midday.
  • 🌵 Cactus Garden (T1 Rooftop) — 100+ desert plant species in tropical Singapore. Unique contrast, quieter crowd, and a bar nearby!
  • 🐟 Orchid Garden & Koi Pond (T2) — 30+ orchid species by theme. Serene and beautiful. Speak softly here, people treat it as a rest space.
  • 🎮 Entertainment Deck (T2) — Free PlayStation and Xbox consoles, movie screening corners. Great for long layovers and families.
  • 🎬 Free Movie Theatre (T2 & T3) — Yes, actual cinema, free movies, no catch. 24 hours.
  • 🌸 Dreamscape (T2) — Digital sky ceilings, water elements, 20,000+ plants. Part of Changi's next-generation vision.

😴 Sleeping Airside (Overnight Layover)

  • Snooze Lounges — The official free rest zones. At all 4 terminals, open 24 hours. All Snooze Lounge locations →
  • Movie theatres and quiet zones work as unofficial backup options
  • Sleeping in transit areas is allowed and common
Important — if you have checked luggage: Don't go through immigration and then try to exit from a different terminal. Each terminal has its own Lost & Found. If bags are removed from the belt (which happens after some time), you must exit through your arrival terminal to collect them. You cannot re-enter airside once you've passed through immigration.

🧳 Luggage Storage

Arriving early or have a late checkout? Luggage storage is available at all terminals and inside Jewel — hourly, daily, and oversized options. Drop bags at the Jewel counter before exploring so you're hands-free.

💡 Early Arrival Tip Many people ask what they can do when they arrive early in the morning and need to wait for the hotel check-in time. Our recommendation is that you go to your hotel and drop your bags off at the concierge, and start your holiday hands free. Check with your hotel in advance to see if this is OK with them, but most hotels are fine with this so why hold yourself back and lose half a day exploring? :)
✈️ Departing? Skip the Storage Queue! If you have an early morning flight or a flight later in the day, consider using Changi's Early Check-In (ECI) service instead of paying for luggage storage. Check in your bags at the Jewel Early Check-In Lounge (Level 1, Jewel), and then explore the airport and Jewel completely hands-free — no storage fees needed! Over 20 airlines participate including Singapore Airlines, Emirates, Qantas, KLM, and more. Check with your airline if they offer this — it's free and it means you can head straight to your gate after enjoying Jewel's facilities. Details & airlines →

🛂 Do I need a Visa?

Singapore takes border control seriously, but the process is typically fast and professional. Most nationalities enjoy visa-free entry for 30–90 days — always check the latest requirements before travel. Check if you need a Visa here →

📋 Is a Visa and SG Arrival Card the same thing?

No — they are not the same thing. A Visa is a separate document that some nationalities need to obtain before travelling to Singapore. An SG Arrival Card, on the other hand, is required by everyone regardless of nationality — it is part of the standard entry process. It is FREE and must only be submitted through the official Immigration & Checkpoints Authority (ICA) site: eservices.ica.gov.sg/sgarrivalcard. Do not use third-party websites that charge for this — the official submission is always free.

🧾 What do I need to enter Singapore?

  • Passport valid for at least 6 months
  • Visa (if required — check the ICA visa requirements page for your nationality)
  • Completed SG Arrival Card (submitted online — free, official ICA site only)
  • Return or onward ticket (sometimes checked)
  • Accommodation address (sometimes checked)

🚫 Customs — Things Tourists Get Caught Out By

  • Bringing large quantities of cigarettes without declaration (duty-free limit: 1 litre spirits, 1 litre wine or beer — no tobacco duty-free allowance since 2023!)
  • Importing chewing gum in bulk (only medicinal/dental gum allowed)
  • 🚭 No vaping — at all! E-cigarettes, vapes, and similar devices are completely banned in Singapore. This includes bringing them in for personal use. Possession, use, and importation are all illegal and can result in heavy fines or prosecution.
  • Bringing goods purchased overseas into Singapore: All new goods bought abroad are subject to Singapore GST upon arrival. If you've been away more than 48 hours, you get GST import relief up to S$500; if less than 48 hours, the limit is S$100. Goods above your relief limit must be declared at the Red Channel. Importantly, removing price tags or packaging does not exempt goods from tax. Gifts are also included in the total value. If you're buying expensive items like designer handbags or luxury goods overseas and bringing them into Singapore, be aware that customs may assess their value even without a receipt.
  • Goods intended for resale: GST import relief only covers goods for personal use. If you are purchasing goods abroad to resell in Singapore, these do not qualify for personal GST relief and must be declared. Singapore Customs takes a serious view of under-declaration — failure to declare can result in fines or prosecution.
  • Carrying prohibited drugs or certain medications without a letter from your doctor — check HSA guidelines here →
  • Bringing counterfeit goods
If in doubt, use the Red Channel. Singapore values honesty in declarations. Penalties for false declarations are heavy.
⚠️ IMPORTANT — Zero Tolerance for Bribery: Do not offer money or gifts to any official — Singapore has some of the world's strictest anti-corruption laws. Even a small gesture that could be perceived as a bribe can lead to immediate arrest — not just at the airport, but anywhere in Singapore. This is taken extremely seriously.
💰 GST Refund — Tourist Refund Scheme (TRS) Singapore charges 9% GST on most goods. The good news: as a tourist you can claim most of it back on eligible shopping! Here's how it works:

✅ To be eligible: You must spend at least S$100 (including GST) in a single day at a participating TRS retailer (look for the "Tax Free" or eTRS logo in the window — not every shop participates). Present your passport in person at the time of purchase so the retailer can log your details electronically.

🏬 Participating stores include major department stores, luxury brands, electronics retailers and many tourist-facing shops — but not every store qualifies. Always confirm with the cashier before paying.

❌ What you cannot claim GST back on:
  • Services — hotel stays, spa treatments, car hire, tours, dining, entertainment
  • Food and beverages consumed in Singapore
  • Goods already used, opened, or consumed in Singapore
  • Purchases from non-participating retailers
🛂 At the airport: Before departure, use the eTRS self-help kiosks (in the departure halls of all four terminals — before or after immigration depending on whether your goods are checked or carry-on). Scan your passport, confirm your purchases, and choose your refund method. You'll typically get back around 7–8% after a small processing fee. Refund by credit card takes up to 10 days; cash is immediate at the GST counter after immigration.

Full details on the IRAS website →

🏙️ First Steps in the City

Singapore uses its own currency, the Singapore Dollar (S$). We do not use other currencies such as USD. Most people get around very easily cashless — cards are accepted almost everywhere: malls, restaurants, and taxis (though taxis may add a small admin fee for card payments). Hawker centres and small local shops may still be cash-only, so it's worth having some notes on you.

💱 Currency Exchange

Airport rates are convenient but slightly worse than in the city. That said, because Singapore is mostly cashless, the amount you'd typically need to change may not be that much — so it might not be worth travelling all around the city hunting for the best rate.

For reference you can check current rates at cashchanger.co/singapore. Money changers are usually not far from you — search Google Maps to find your nearest one. If you're in a shopping mall, they're often tucked away in the basement or on the top floor in some obscure corner.

Rates at hotels and banks aren't usually as competitive as money changers — worth knowing before you queue.

🏧 Cash & ATMs

The best approach is to bring your home currency and change cash at a money changer as and when you need it. For reference: a hawker meal is around S$8–$12, and a taxi ride between S$15–$25 (though most taxis also take card with a small additional fee).

  • ATMs are widely available at MRT stations, malls, banks, and 7-Eleven
  • Most accept international Visa/Mastercard
  • Notify your home bank before travel to avoid card blocks

📲 Contactless Payments

Singapore is extremely cashless-friendly. Apple Pay, Google Pay, PayNow, and contactless cards work at most places. The MRT accepts contactless bank cards directly — no EZ-Link card needed if you have a Visa or Mastercard.

💡 At the shops The attendant may ask you "Using?" — that's their way of asking how you're paying. Your reply would be "Paywave" or "Cash". Or they might say "Paywave?" and you just say "Yes" and tap the payment console. We keep conversations short here — it's not rude, it's just how we talk. :)

🚇 MRT — Cheapest Option

The Changi Airport MRT station sits between T2 and T3. Take the train to Tanah Merah, then transfer to the main East-West Line heading into the city. Total journey is around 30–40 minutes and costs around S$2.00–$2.50.

First train: ~5:31am  |  Last train: ~11:18pm. Outside these hours, take a taxi or Grab.

🚕 Taxis

Available 24/7. Just follow the overhead yellow signs in the arrival hall to the taxi stand — the queue moves quickly and you'll be directed to the next waiting cab. Taxis are metered and driven by local Singaporeans who rely on this as their livelihood, so you're unlikely to be taken for a ride. If you're ever concerned, ask for a receipt before you get in — it'll show the taxi number, distance, time, and any surcharges, which covers you if there's ever a dispute.

Budget around S$25–S$40 to the city centre. A few things worth knowing:

  • Airport surcharge: S$5–8 added on top of the metered fare for airport pickups
  • Peak hour surcharges: 25% surcharge Mon–Fri 6–9:30am and Mon–Sun 6pm–midnight. Off-peak (midday and early afternoon) is the cheapest time to take a taxi.
  • Late night surcharge: 50% surcharge midnight–6am
  • Change of shift: You may see a taxi with a destination sign like "Tampines" or "Woodlands" — this means the driver is heading back at end of shift and will only take passengers going roughly in that direction. It's not personal, just wave the next one down.
  • "On Call" sign: the taxi has been pre-booked and is on its way to collect someone else — not available for hailing.
  • "Busy" sign: confusingly, this often means the driver is on a break or not taking passengers — not that they're occupied. Wave the next one.
  • Identifying available taxis: Look for the illuminated roof sign — if it's lit, the taxi is free. Taxis come in different colours from different companies, but pricing is broadly the same.

You generally don't need to pre-book from the airport — just join the queue. From your hotel, ask the concierge or flag one from the street. Taxis also have their own booking app called CDG Zig.

📱 Grab — Private Hire Vehicle (PHV)

Grab is our version of Uber, available across several Southeast Asian countries, so installing it before you travel is well worth it. It has the largest fleet of private hire vehicles in Singapore — look for the blue PHV sticker on the front or rear windscreen. Beyond rides, you can also order food delivery from the same app.

Pickup zones at the airport are separate from the taxi stands — follow signs for "Private Hire Vehicles." Be early — drivers often can't stop for long, and going around for another pass adds 10 minutes. Payment is cashless via card (we prefer Wise), so when you arrive, just open the door and go — it's already been paid. No haggling, no surprises — the price was fixed when you booked.

💡 Group & Luggage Tip A standard taxi or Grab seats 4 passengers and fits 2 large bags and 1 cabin bag. If you need more room, book a "transfer" — pickup is also at the PHV zone and prices range from around S$40–$60. Need a trusted driver? Let us know and we'll arrange an introduction — they have larger vehicles too.
👨‍👩‍👧 Travelling with young children? GrabFamily
GrabFamily pairs you with a driver who has a child seat fitted. Child seat use is compulsory for children under 1.35m in private hire vehicles. Taxis are exempt from this rule under Singapore law — you can use a taxi without a child seat, though it's always safer to have one. Seatbelts are compulsory for all passengers, front and back, in every vehicle.

🏨 Hotel Shuttle

Some hotels offer shared or private airport shuttles — great if you have heavy luggage or your hotel isn't near an MRT. Check with your hotel at the time of booking.

🚇 Getting Around

The MRT is the backbone of Singapore transport. It's clean, air-conditioned, punctual, and runs through the whole island. If a train is even a few minutes late, announcements are made — that's how reliable the system is.

🕒 Operating Hours

  • First trains: from about 5:30am
  • Last trains: around 11:30pm–midnight (varies by line and station)
  • Trains run every 2–5 minutes during peak hours
  • After midnight — take Grab or taxi!
🔔 2026 Note: Circle Line passengers should expect delays of up to 30 mins during peak hours until April 19, 2026, due to tunnel strengthening works at Mountbatten, Dakota & Paya Lebar stations. Shuttle buses are available.

🗺️ Key Tourist Stations

  • Bayfront (CC/DT) — Marina Bay Sands, Gardens by the Bay, Art Science Museum
  • HarbourFront (NE/CC) — Sentosa, VivoCity Mall
  • Orchard (NS) — Shopping district
  • Chinatown (NE/DT) — Heritage temples, Maxwell Food Centre
  • Bugis (EW/DT) — Bugis Street, Kampong Glam, Haji Lane
  • City Hall (NS/EW) — Esplanade, Raffles Hotel, Padang
  • Little India (NE/DT) — Cultural district, Mustafa Centre
  • Mandai (Thomson) — Singapore Zoo, Night Safari, Bird Paradise

📏 Unwritten MRT Rules (Follow These!)

  • No eating or drinking — including chewing gum. Fines apply.
  • Queue behind the marked lines on the platform, let passengers exit first
  • Move inside the carriage — don't block the doors
  • Escalators: stand left, walk right — this is taken very seriously!
  • Give up priority seats (marked in yellow) for elderly, pregnant, disabled
  • Backpacks off your back in crowded trains — hold in front
  • Headphones for videos/music — don't play on speaker

💳 How to Pay

  • EZ-Link card — buy at any station, top up at MRT machines or 7-Eleven. Most convenient for tourists.
  • Tourist Pass — unlimited rides for 1/2/3 days from SGD$22
  • Contactless bank card — tap in/out with Visa or Mastercard directly. Foreign cards incur a S$0.60 admin fee per day.
  • Google Pay / Apple Pay — accepted at all fare gates and buses

👶 Free Travel for Young Children

All children under 7 years old travel free on basic bus and MRT services. Here's how it works:
  • Under 0.9m tall: Travel free automatically — no card needed. Just accompany a fare-paying adult. Staff at the gates will wave them through.
  • 0.9m–1.2m tall & under 7 years old: Eligible for a Child Concession Card — which gives free travel on all basic bus and MRT services. Without it, the full adult fare applies.
  • Valid until: 30 April of the year the child turns 7, regardless of birth month.

📋 How to Get the Child Concession Card

  • Visit any SimplyGo Ticket Office inside an MRT station — no appointment needed. Common locations: Orchard, City Hall, Jurong East, Tampines, Changi Airport.
  • Bring your child's passport (for non-Singapore citizens) or birth certificate
  • Bring your own passport or ID as the accompanying adult
  • The card is free — no cost to apply, no deposit
  • The card is ready immediately — no waiting, no activation required
  • Each child is entitled to one card only
💡 Tourist Tip If your child is over 0.9m but under 7, it's absolutely worth the 10-minute stop at any MRT station to get this card. It saves the full adult fare on every single journey. A week of sightseeing means many MRT trips — this adds up quickly!
⚠️ Express & Premium Services: The Child Concession Card gives free travel on all regular MRT lines and standard buses. For Premium Bus Services and NightRider services, the card must be topped up and the adult cash fare applies.

Singapore's bus network covers the entire island including neighbourhoods not on the MRT. Buses are modern, air-conditioned, and display next stops on digital screens. The challenge for tourists is knowing which number to take — use Google Maps!

Tap in AND tap out! Always use your card when boarding AND when leaving the bus. If you forget to tap out, the system charges the maximum fare for that route. That's an expensive mistake on a hot day! 🥵
  • Use Google Maps for real-time bus directions and arrival times
  • Move to the back of the bus after boarding — don't crowd the door
  • Bus stops show route numbers and estimated arrival times
  • Signal the driver by pressing the stop bell early enough

📱 Grab (Most Popular)

Grab is Singapore's dominant ride-hailing app — think of it as Uber, but for Southeast Asia. Book cars, taxis, large vehicles, and even food delivery. Upfront pricing, cashless, and you can see the driver's details. Download before you fly!

📱 Gojek

Gojek is Grab's main competitor. Worth comparing prices during surge periods — sometimes Gojek is cheaper when Grab is busy.

Local Food Explained 🍜
Singapore's food is legendary. Here's what to eat, where to eat it, and how to order like you've lived here for years. 😄
🏪 Hawker Centres — The Heart of Singapore Food
UNESCO Heritage! Singapore hawker culture is on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list. This is where locals eat daily — multiple meals, affordable, and often Michelin-quality. Don't leave without eating at one!

Hawker centres are open-air food courts where individual stall operators cook one speciality — often perfected over decades. A single meal costs S$3–S$8. No waiters, no dress code, pure food culture.

🪑 The "Chope" Seat System

A packet of tissues, an umbrella, or a small personal item on a table means it's reserved. This is called "chope-ing" a seat. It's a uniquely Singaporean social contract — don't move someone's packet of tissues! During busy peak hours, strangers share tables and that's totally normal.

📋 How to Order

  • Walk the stalls to see what's on offer, then choose
  • Queue at the stall you want. Be ready to order quickly during peak hours — hesitation is frowned upon!
  • Many stalls ask: "Take away or eat here?"
  • Some stalls are cash-only. Have small notes ready (S$2, $5).
  • Collect your food when called or when ready
  • Return your tray to the tray return station when done. Mandatory at most food courts, and socially enforced at hawker centres too.
  • Only take utensils from the stall you're ordering from. Chopsticks, spoons, and forks belong to that stall — don't grab them from a neighbouring stall you haven't ordered from.
  • Many stalls close on Mondays — they work straight through weekends and take Monday as their rest day. If a stall you specifically want to visit is closed, that's likely why. Plan accordingly!

🍚 Economy Rice — How to Order

Every hawker centre and food court has an Economy Rice stall — it's the one with a spread of pre-cooked dishes on display that you point and choose from. The lines are usually long but move fast, so know what you want before you get to the front.

  • Step 1 — Eat here or takeaway? The first thing they'll ask. Have your answer ready: "Eat here" or "Takeaway".
  • Step 2 — Rice portion. If you don't want white rice, say "No rice" or "Half rice" right after you answer Step 1. This is the moment to say it.
  • Step 3 — Point at your dishes. Just point. "This one" and "that one" — no need to name anything. Their English may be limited and pointing is completely normal and not rude at all. That's just how it works.
  • What to order: Most people get 1–2 meat dishes and 1–2 vegetable dishes with rice. Whole pieces of meat or fish are premium — sometimes S$3 or more per piece. While in the queue, scan the display and decide mentally. The line moves fast and indecisiveness will meet impatience.
  • Step 4 — Sauce? At the end they may ask if you want sauce on your rice. Yes or no. Simple.
  • Step 5 — Pay. Have cash ready. S$10 is usually fine. Handing over a S$50 note will likely get you a look and a "do you have smaller?"

  • Lau Pa Sat Satay Street — The road closes to traffic from 7pm for outdoor satay stalls. Atmospheric and one of Singapore's best evening experiences.

Halal food is incredibly easy to find — Singapore is about 15% Muslim and Halal certification is taken very seriously across the island.

🕌 What Halal Means

Halal is an Arabic word meaning "permissible" under Islamic law. For food, this means the ingredients, preparation, and handling must all meet specific religious standards — it's not just about avoiding pork or alcohol. A Halal-certified stall is audited and approved by MUIS (Majlis Ugama Islam Singapura), Singapore's Islamic religious authority.

🍽️ Utensils, Trays & Separation Matter

One thing many visitors don't realise: cross-contamination is a serious concern. At Halal stalls, the utensils, cooking surfaces, and even the trays are kept separate from non-Halal ones. If you're eating at a Halal stall, please be mindful:

  • Don't bring non-Halal food (e.g. pork dishes from another stall) and eat it at a Halal stall's table area
  • Return trays to the correct tray return station — Halal and non-Halal trays are separated in many hawker centres. Look for the signs.
  • Don't use the same utensils across Halal and non-Halal food

This matters because cross-contamination — even indirect — compromises the Halal status of food for Muslim diners. A little awareness goes a long way in showing respect.

✅ Finding Halal Food

  • Look for the MUIS Halal Certification logo on stall signage
  • All Malay hawker stalls and most Indian hawker stalls are Halal by default
  • Major hawker centres have plenty of Halal options — Tekka Centre (Little India) is especially well-stocked
  • Use the Halal SG app or HalalFinder.sg to locate certified restaurants near you
  • McDonald's, KFC, Subway, and most major fast food chains in Singapore are Halal-certified
  • Staples like Nasi Lemak, Satay, Roti Prata, Mee Goreng, and Nasi Goreng are all Halal and available everywhere
Understanding Locals
Culture & Avoiding Conflict 🤝
Singapore has very specific social norms, laws, and unwritten rules. Understanding these helps you blend in, avoid awkward situations, and genuinely connect with locals.
🧭 Culture & Behaviour

Singaporeans are often misread as cold or unfriendly by first-time visitors. They're not — they're efficient, private, and reserved in public. Once you understand the cultural context, interactions become much more comfortable.

  • "Mind your own business" culture — Singaporeans avoid interfering in others' matters. Being overly inquisitive about salary, religion, or personal life with strangers feels intrusive.
  • Public composure matters — Displaying strong emotions (shouting, dramatic reactions) is seen as loss of self-control. Calm, composed behaviour earns respect.
  • Efficiency over small talk — In shops, taxis, and food stalls, expect functional communication rather than chattiness. This isn't rudeness — it's cultural efficiency.
  • Friendliness develops slowly — Singaporeans may seem reserved initially but warm up considerably over time. Don't push it.
  • "Sorry" is used casually — People say "sorry" even for minor situations like brushing past someone. It's politeness, not admission of serious fault.
  • Group harmony first — Singaporeans often prioritise not causing inconvenience to others over personal preference. This is why the queuing and escalator etiquette is taken so seriously.
🦁 Kiasu Culture "Kiasu" is a Hokkien word meaning "afraid to lose out." It explains a lot about Singaporean behaviour — queueing early for popular hawker stalls, booking things far in advance, grabbing freebies quickly. It's both self-aware and a genuine cultural trait. Singaporeans often joke about being kiasu themselves!

Singlish is a uniquely Singaporean creole — English mixed with Malay, Hokkien, Cantonese, and Tamil. English is perfectly understood everywhere, but hearing Singlish is part of the cultural experience!

  • Don't block MRT doors. Move inside the carriage. Standing in the doorway frustrates everyone.
  • Phone volume: Use headphones. Playing videos or calls on speaker is one of the most complained-about behaviours. Keep the volume for your ears only — the person next to you doesn't want to know what happened to Aunty Val.
  • Backpacks off in crowded trains — hold in front or place on the floor between your legs.
  • Priority seats: Avoid them if someone in need is visible. Even if they're empty, sitting there can attract silent judgment.
  • Queue at platform doors: Yellow line markings show where to stand. Queue beside the door markers — don't cut in.
  • Keep conversations between yourselves. Singaporeans generally keep to themselves on public transport — they're not unfriendly, just private. Keep your conversation volume to your group, not the whole carriage, and don't spread out across seats or handrails in a way that takes up the space of others.
  • Exiting the station: When tapping out, use the same card you tapped in with. Tapping a different card won't let you through the gantry — and yes, this includes accidentally tapping your entire wallet at the reader. One card at a time at the exit.

Singapore is religiously diverse — Buddhism, Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and Taoism all coexist. Religious sites are active places of worship, not just tourist attractions.

  • Dress modestly at all temples and mosques — shoulders and knees covered. Many places lend sarongs or robes at the entrance.
  • Remove shoes before entering Hindu temples and mosques. Look for shoe racks at the entrance.
  • Ask before photographing inside. Some areas and rituals are restricted.
  • Head covering for women at mosques may be required — scarves are often available to borrow at the entrance.
  • Halal awareness: When eating with Muslim friends, ensure food is Halal-certified. This is a matter of deep respect, not inconvenience.
⚠️ No Casual Religious Jokes Singapore's Maintenance of Religious Harmony Act is real and actively enforced. Comments or jokes that demean or mock any religion — even casually — can have serious legal consequences. This isn't a quirk; it's a cornerstone of how Singapore keeps its multicultural society peaceful. Simply put: don't go there.
Singapore takes its laws seriously. Ignorance is not accepted as an excuse. The fines are real and the enforcement is genuine. That said — it's extremely easy to follow these rules, and they're why Singapore is so pleasant!
#1
Littering — Zero Tolerance
Even dropping a cigarette butt or small wrapper leads to fines starting at S$300. Repeat offenders face corrective work orders (cleaning public spaces in bright vest). Don't do it.
#2
Chewing Gum Ban
Importing or selling chewing gum is banned (except dental/medical gum with a prescription). You can bring in personal amounts for your own use, but selling it is illegal. Don't try to bring in large quantities.
#3
No Vaping / E-Cigarettes
Vapes are completely banned in Singapore — possession, use, import, and sale are all illegal. Fines of up to S$2,000 for first-time offenders. Don't bring them into Singapore.
#4
Eating/Drinking on MRT
Fines of up to S$500. No food, drinks, or chewing gum inside trains and stations — and this includes water. Not even a closed bottle of water is technically permitted. 💡 Side tip: Use the toilet before you board. Although rare, trains do occasionally get delayed between stations, and you don't want to be stuck in that situation.
#5
Smoking Restrictions
Smoking is banned in most public places — hawker centres, parks, bus stops, within 5m of building entrances. Designated Smoking Areas (DSAs) are marked with a yellow box on the ground, usually positioned away from main foot traffic and often without shelter. Fine: S$1,000 (first offence). Enforcement officers actively patrol.
#6
Public Drinking Restrictions
There are two separate rules here that are easy to mix up. Retail outlets (convenience stores, supermarkets, petrol stations) cannot sell takeaway alcohol after 10:30pm. However, licensed restaurants and bars can serve alcohol to seated patrons until midnight — so dining in at a restaurant past 10:30pm with a drink is perfectly fine. What is banned island-wide between 10:30pm and 7am is consuming alcohol in public spaces (parks, void decks, streets, sidewalks). Liquor Control Zones (Little India and Geylang) have stricter rules — no public drinking on weekends and public holidays from 10:30pm Friday through 7am Monday. Fines up to S$1,000.
#7
Drug Laws — Extreme Penalties
Singapore has zero tolerance for drugs. Even marijuana (legal in your home country) is illegal here. Possession = jail. Trafficking can carry the death penalty. Do not bring any controlled substances into Singapore. Period.
#8
Not Flushing Public Toilets
Public toilet inspectors exist and do issue fines for not flushing. Singapore's public toilets are generally very clean — help keep them that way!
#9
Feeding Pigeons & Wild Animals
Feeding pigeons is illegal and carries fines. Same goes for wild animals in parks. Feeding monkeys at MacRitchie is specifically prohibited — they can become aggressive.
#10
Drone Flying Without Permit
Flying drones in restricted areas (most of Singapore is restricted!) without a permit from CAAS is illegal. Even small recreational drones require registration. Don't assume you can fly just because the view is amazing.
#11
Jaywalking
Crossing the road outside a designated crossing within 50m of a traffic crossing is technically illegal. In practice, enforcement varies but be careful near major roads.
💡 Singapore Building Quirk & Reading Addresses Singapore buildings don't have a "G" (Ground) floor — Level 1 is ground level. Some buildings also skip Level 4 and Level 13 (considered unlucky in Chinese culture). Don't be confused!

How to read a Singapore address: Addresses follow a consistent format once you know the pattern.

🏪 Retail / Mall: #03-22 ION Orchard, 2 Orchard Turn, Singapore 238801 — the #03 is the floor (3rd level) and 22 is the unit number. Always look for the # sign — that's your floor and door.

🏠 Shophouse: 26 Ann Siang Road, Singapore 069710 — shophouses are usually 2–3 storey heritage buildings along a street, numbered sequentially. The street number is the building. The famous Zam Zam restaurant at 697–699 North Bridge Road spans two shophouse units.

🏘️ Residential (HDB): Blk 85 Bedok North Road, #04-123, Singapore 460085 — Blk = Block number (the building), #04 = 4th floor, 123 = the flat number. HDB blocks are everywhere and each block has a large number displayed on the outside.

Postal codes in Singapore are 6 digits and are very precise — often pointing to a single building or block. If you have the postal code, Google Maps will get you there exactly.
🌡️ Dress Light Unless you're attending a formal or official occasion, dress comfortably — it's the norm here. Tourists in shorts and a t-shirt are completely at home, and nobody bats an eyelid. Crocs, runners, and sandals are all fine. Mainstream casual wear won't set off any alarms.

Pack one collared shirt and a pair of jeans just in case you end up somewhere that has a smarter dress code (some rooftop bars or nicer restaurants), but the overwhelming majority of your trip won't need it.

Do bring a light jacket — not for the weather, but for the indoors. Singapore's shopping mall air conditioning can be Arctic-level cold, and the Cloud Forest at Gardens by the Bay is intentionally chilly. One thin layer makes a big difference.
💰 Singapore is Expensive Don't assume "Asian country = cheap." Singapore is consistently one of the most expensive cities in the world. Budget accordingly — especially for accommodation, alcohol, and tourist experiences. Hawker food is cheap; cocktail bars are not!
🗺️ Distances Are Deceptive Singapore looks small on a map — and it is — but don't underestimate how much walking is involved. The MRT covers most of it, but getting from the station to the actual attraction often means 10–20 minutes on foot, in heat and humidity. Don't be surprised if you clock 15,000 to 20,000 steps in a day. It's very common.

Wear comfortable shoes, keep water with you, and plan your route to cluster nearby attractions together rather than zigzagging across the island.
♿ Accessibility & Mobility Singapore is well equipped for visitors with mobility needs. Ramps, lifts, and wide pathways are standard throughout the MRT network, shopping malls, and most tourist areas. Wheelchairs navigate comfortably almost everywhere. The MRT has dedicated spaces and staff assistance is available at stations.

One caveat: during peak hours (7–9am and 5:30–7:30pm on weekdays), MRT carriages get crowded and boarding can be difficult. Where possible, plan around peak times if mobility is a concern.
🤝 Bargaining Culture Unlike some Asian countries, bargaining is generally NOT practised except at specific markets (Chinatown street market, some shops in Bugis). In malls and most shops, prices are fixed. Don't try to bargain — it's awkward and rarely works.

✅ Safety Tips
  • Singapore is extremely safe — violent crime is very rare. Normal travel safety precautions are sufficient.
  • Keep a photocopy or phone photo of your passport, travel insurance, and flight details.
  • Buy comprehensive travel insurance before you go — medical costs without it can be high.
  • Save these emergency numbers in your phone before you land.
  • Tap water is 100% safe to drink anywhere in Singapore.
  • There are no significant health risks specific to Singapore for most visitors. Standard vaccinations are sufficient.
  • Theft is uncommon but pickpocketing can occur in crowded areas like markets — keep bags zipped.
